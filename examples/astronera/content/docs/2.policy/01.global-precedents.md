ALAN regulation is an established global practice. Over 40 countries across five continents have formally recognised ALAN in law and implemented enforceable controls through planning, environmental, and sectoral frameworks.

These precedents eliminate implementation risk. Maharashtra can adopt tested frameworks, adapt proven standards, and leverage two decades of international regulatory experience.

## Regulatory Model Types

Global jurisdictions approach ALAN regulation through five primary models, each with distinct legal mechanisms and enforcement structures.

### I. Explicit National Recognition

Countries where ALAN is legally defined as pollution with comprehensive statutory frameworks.

| Country | Year | Legal Status | Key Controls | Enforcement |
|---------|------|--------------|--------------|-------------|
| **Croatia** | 2019 | Environmental harm (statute) | Illumination limits, zoning, protected areas, planning controls | :cite[Mandatory compliance]{#croatia-2019-light-pollution-law} |
| **France** | 2010, 2018 | Pollution (decree) | <1% upward light, :dfn[CCT]{#cct} ≤3000K, lumen caps, skybeam ban, curfews | :cite[Public and private binding]{#france-2018-light-nuisance-decree} |
| **Slovenia** | 2007 | Pollution (decree) | 0% upward light, 4-zone system, lumen caps, full shielding | :cite[Strict zoning enforcement]{#slovenia-2007-light-limitation} |
| **South Korea** | 2012, 2019 | Harmful lighting (act) | 4-zone classes, CCT ≤3000K, 10pm curfews, 5-year local plans | :cite[Mandatory local planning]{#korea-2012-light-pollution-act} |
| **Chile** | 2022 | Pollution (decree) | :dfn[Full shielding]{#full-cutoff-fixture}, CCT ≤3000K, observatory buffers, phased retrofits | :cite[Phased compliance]{#chile-2022-light-emission-standard} |
| **Mexico** | 2021 | Pollutant (LGEEPA) | Special zones, CCT/intensity limits via permits | :cite[Permit-based control]{#mexico-2021-lgeepa-amendment} |

::tip
**Maharashtra Relevance**: These frameworks demonstrate that ALAN can be regulated as environmental pollution under existing state environmental authority, similar to air or water quality standards.
::

### II. Implicit National Recognition

Countries where ALAN is regulated through general environmental law without specific statutory definition.

| Country | Year | Legal Basis | Key Approach | Implementation |
|---------|------|-------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Niue** | 2007 | Dark Sky Nation | Nation-wide dark-sky lighting standards | Ministry and Dark Sky Committee oversight |
| **Pakistan** | 1997 | Air pollutant (PEPA) | National Environmental Quality Standards and EIA | :cite[Case-by-case requirements]{#pakistan-1997-pepa} |
| **Switzerland** | 1983 | Non-ionising radiation | Precautionary principle | :cite[Non-binding guidance]{#switzerland-1983-environment-act} |

::tip
**Maharashtra Relevance**: Demonstrates flexibility to regulate ALAN under existing environmental protection frameworks without requiring dedicated legislation.
::

### III. Regional Leadership

Sub-national models directly relevant to Indian states with constitutional authority over environmental matters.

| Region | Year | Legal Mechanism | Key Standards | Applicability |
|--------|------|----------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Italy (Regions)** | 2000, 2021 | Regional statutes and UNI 10819:2021 national standard | Low/zero upward light, shielding, lumen caps, observatory buffers | :cite[Regional mandatory enforcement]{#italy-2000-regional-laws} |
| **Austria (Provinces)** | 2024 | Landscape protection acts | 10pm–6am switch-offs, warm lighting, beam limits, retrofits | :cite[Provincial enforcement]{#austria-2024-landscape-act} |

::tip
**Maharashtra Relevance**: **High applicability**. Regional/provincial models demonstrate state-level regulatory authority and district-level implementation, matching India's federal structure.
::

### IV. Integrated Policy

:dfn[Astrotourism]{#astrotourism}-led dark sky governance models linking conservation with economic development.

| Country | Year | Policy Framework | Economic Linkage | Governance Structure |
|---------|------|-----------------|------------------|---------------------|
| **South Africa** | 2024 | National astro-tourism strategy | Dark-sky and science infrastructure integration | Cabinet-approved strategy |
| **New Zealand** | 2023, 2025 | Protected resource (RMA) | Starlight protection in tourism zones | :cite[District plan binding]{#newzealand-2023-mackenzie-starlight} |

::tip
**Maharashtra Relevance**: **High applicability**. Provides model for linking dark sky conservation with tourism policy, demonstrating economic returns alongside environmental protection.
::

### V. Sector-Specific

Targeted approaches applicable via departmental mandates without comprehensive legislation.

| Country | Legal Instrument | Sector Focus | Key Standards | Department Alignment |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Spain** | Observatory protection law (1988, 2008) | Astronomy infrastructure | No light above horizon (Canaries), :dfn[glare]{#glare} limits | :cite[Tourism, Science]{#spain-1988-observatory-protection} |
| **USA** | Municipal ordinances | Local control | Shielding, spectrum limits, lumen caps | :cite[Urban Development]{#newyork-2014-darksky-compliant} |
| **Germany** | Biodiversity statute (2021) | Nature conservation | Insect-friendly lighting, nature reserve restrictions | :cite[Forest, Environment]{#bavaria-2021-light-protection} |
| **UK** | Statutory nuisance (2006) | Complaint-driven | Case-by-case enforcement, planning guidance | :cite[Law, Urban Development]{#uk-2005-statutory-nuisance} |
| **Malta** | Ecosystem and advertising regulations (2016, 2020) | Dual focus | Lumen caps, CCT ≤3000K, shielding, Natura 2000 sites zero spill | :cite[Environment, Commerce]{#malta-2016-light-regulations} |
| **Netherlands** | Duty of care (2024) | General environmental duty | Prevent nuisance (no numeric caps) | :cite[Environment]{#netherlands-2024-environment-act} |

::tip
**Maharashtra Relevance**: Demonstrates that departments can regulate ALAN within existing mandates (tourism, forest, urban planning) without waiting for comprehensive framework.
::

## Comparative Analysis

### Legal Authority Pathways

Maharashtra can exercise ALAN regulatory authority through multiple pathways:

| Pathway | Legal Basis | Precedent Countries | Implementation Timeline |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Environmental Pollution** | Maharashtra Environment Protection Act | Croatia, France, Slovenia | 12-18 months (notification process) |
| **Tourism Asset Protection** | Tourism Policy amendment | South Africa, New Zealand | 6-9 months (policy revision) |
| **Protected Area Management** | Wildlife Protection Act, Forest Act | Germany, Spain | Immediate (existing authority) |
| **Urban Planning** | Municipal Acts, Development Plans | Italy, Austria, USA | 12-24 months (plan amendments) |

### Enforcement Mechanisms

Global precedents demonstrate diverse enforcement approaches:

**Proactive Compliance**:
- France: Automatic curfews, fixture standards in building codes
- Slovenia: Pre-approval of all outdoor lighting installations
- South Korea: 5-year municipal lighting plans with oversight

**Reactive Enforcement**:
- UK: Statutory nuisance complaints to local authorities
- USA: Municipal code violations with penalties
- Switzerland: Non-binding guidance with voluntary compliance

**Hybrid Approach** (recommended for Maharashtra):
- Proactive standards for new development and government projects
- Reactive enforcement for existing private installations
- Phased retrofit mandates for public infrastructure

## Key Lessons for Maharashtra

### What Works

1. **Phased Implementation**: Chile and France demonstrate successful multi-year rollout with clear deadlines
2. **Zone-Based Standards**: Slovenia's 4-zone system balances protection with development needs
3. **Pilot Projects**: South Africa and New Zealand validate approaches before scaling
4. **Tourism Integration**: Astrotourism linkage creates constituency for enforcement
5. **Technical Standards**: Adoption of international standards (:dfn[DSI]{#dsi}, CIE) reduces development costs

### What to Avoid

1. **Non-Binding Guidelines**: Switzerland's voluntary approach shows limited effectiveness
2. **Complaint-Only Enforcement**: UK model creates inconsistent outcomes
3. **No Numeric Standards**: Netherlands' general duty of care lacks measurability
4. **Single-Sector Focus**: Germany's insect-only approach misses broader benefits

## Recommended Model for Maharashtra

Based on global precedents, Maharashtra should adopt a **hybrid regulatory framework**:

**Legal Foundation**: Environmental pollution notification (Croatia/France model)
**Tourism Integration**: Astrotourism strategy (South Africa/New Zealand model)
**Implementation**: Regional phased rollout (Italy/Austria model)
**Enforcement**: Proactive for public sector, hybrid for private (France model)
**Standards**: Zone-based with international technical alignment (Slovenia model)

This combination leverages proven successes while adapting to Maharashtra's governance structure, economic priorities, and implementation capacity.
